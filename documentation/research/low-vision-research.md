## What is Low Vision?

Low vision is **not** total blindness. It describes a permanent affliction to one's sight that cannot be fully corrected with glasses, contacts, medication, or surgery. It occupies the spectrum between normal vision and total blindness.

---

## Measuring Visual Acuity: The Snellen Fraction

Visual acuity (the sharpness of vision) is measured using the **Snellen fraction**.

| Notation | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 20/20 | See clearly at 20 ft what normal eyesight sees at 20 ft (baseline) |
| 20/40 | See clearly at 20 ft what normal eyesight sees at 40 ft |
| 20/200 | See clearly at 20 ft what normal eyesight sees at 200 ft |

- **20/40** is the minimum acuity required for a standard driver's license.
- The same fraction can be expressed in different notations: e.g., 20/70 = 3/10 = 0.3 = 6/18.

### Legal Blindness in the United States
A person is legally blind when they have:
- **20/200 visual acuity** (sometimes cited as 20/100), **and**
- **20 degrees or less of visual field**

### What the Snellen Fraction Does *Not* Measure
The Snellen fraction is a useful benchmark but has real shortcomings - it misses:

- Peripheral vision
- Color vision
- Depth perception
- Eye coordination and focus (relevant to conditions like dyslexia/dysgraphia)

> Two people with the same visual acuity score may experience very different struggles.

## Types of Low Vision *(non-exhaustive)*

| Type | Description | Related Conditions |
|---|---|---|
| **Central Vision Loss** | Loss of detail in the center of the visual field | Macular degeneration |
| **Peripheral Vision Loss** | Loss of side/edge vision (opposite of central loss) | Glaucoma |
| **Reduced Acuity / Blur** | General blurriness or double vision | Refractive error, cataracts |
| **Contrast & Light Sensitivity** | Difficulty distinguishing colors or seeing in low light; includes color loss and night blindness | Various |

> Those struggling with contrast and light sensitivity often go unnoticed and unaided.

## Everyday Challenges Low Vision Creates

Low vision creates real barriers in daily life. Key challenges include:

- **Reading** - labels, menus, mail, screens; personal finances are especially high-stakes
- **Face/Identity Recognition** - may raise security considerations
- **Navigation & Object Detection** - spotting hazards, locating low-contrast items
- **Fluctuating Vision** - inconsistent environments make adaptation difficult

---

## Audience & Scale

- Approximately **¼ of the world** struggles with some form of visual impairment.
- Because low vision is a wide spectrum, many people don't receive the help they need - they have enough sight to attempt visual tasks, but not enough to succeed reliably.
- Users can benefit from **personalized profiles** built around their specific type(s) of low vision.

---

## Design Philosophy

Low vision is a spectrum, and there are many people who suffer from vision loss in different ways. The goal is to cater to all people - building features that serve as everyday solutions:

- Reading labels, menus, and screens
- Facial/identity recognition (with security considerations in mind)
- Environment descriptions to improve navigation
- Object finders for low-contrast items
- Assistance in low-light conditions

> Features should be rooted in the real types of low vision users experience, which will also inform how AI models are trained for specific user conditions.
